Empowerment through Learning and Awareness:

English Language Development Activities (2014-2016):

After receiving training and orientation, Leaping Boundaries Trainers facilitated English lessons during regular class hours at the madrasahs. The trainers followed the government curriculum i.e. textbooks from the Bangladesh Madrasah Education Board but used creative delivery methods to ensure effective learning and facilitation of the syllabus. Tools such as word games, storytelling, story writing, etc. were used to engage students.

Soft Skills Development (2014-2017):

The Soft Skills training were provided to trainers in partnership with Mind Mechanics in 2014 and 2015 and later Thrive in 2016 and focused on the following:

  • Effective Communication Skills: To prepare the students to communicate effectively, both verbally and non-verbally with people in general.
  • Emotional Intelligence and Change Management: To learn to be aware of one’s emotional responses to various situations and at various times, identify what stimulates their reactions, better deal with unprecedented situations and cope with changes.
  • Critical Thinking and Problem Solving: To improve the quality of thinking and impose intellectual standards on the thinking process, and to introduce them to tools to help solve everyday problems in more efficient ways.
  • Creativity: To introduce them to tools to generate creative ideas and solve everyday problems in creative ways, supplementing creative thinking and problem solving.

Aakashbani- a workshop with Climate Tracker (2016):

This workshop was conducted by Sohara Mehroze Shachi at Madinatul Ulum Women’s Kamil Madrasah where 60 students learnt about the South Asia Hub of Climate Trackers and the basics of climate change. The aim was to foster understanding, concern and drive among the girls about climate change. Various signs of climate change such as drought, flood, cyclone, increase of water level, etc were discussed. They were also educated on how to reduce pollution and have a positive impact on climate.

Enhancement of Creative and Critical Thinking among Female Madrasah Students through Technological Education (2017):

This was a six-month project, supported by the EMK Center, for ensuring that girls in madrasahs develop logical reasoning skills through technological education and are able to use those skills to develop solutions to social problems, thereby dissolving existing negative stereotypes and claiming their positions as active citizens and positive agents of change. After conducting a thorough needs and aptitude assessment among 286 girls in three underprivileged madrasahs (Madinatul Ulum Mohila Kamil Madrasah, Al Amin Islamia Alim Madrasah, and Gawsia Islamia Fazil Madrasah) in Dhaka city, 90 girls, aged 12-14, received workshops on social issues, as well training in computer programming. The workshops on Epilepsy Awareness, Autism Awareness, Road Safety and Women’s Safety were conducted by Identity Inclusion, Buckets Engineer, CriticaLink and বহ্নিশিখা Bonhishikha - unlearn gender, respectively. Eight university students were recruited and trained under THRIVE and The Tech Academy as master computer programming trainers.

Building Resiliency of Women Enrolled in Informal Institutions Through Life Skills Development (2018):

This 6-month activity sought to build capacity of female madrasah students to increase their confidence and resilience to violent extremist ideology. A multi-pronged approach was used to develop the life skills of young female students. USAID’s Obirodh: Road to Tolerance project provided a Fixed Amount Award with In-Kind assistance to Shammo Foundation to identify and train 28 volunteers from private/public universities who were deployed to 5 madrasahs (Madinatul Ulum Women’s Kamil Madrasah, Al Amin Islamia Alim Madrasah, Gawsia Islamia Fazil Madrasah, Mohius Sunnah Mohila Madrasah, and Hazratbagh Islamia Alim Madrasah) in Dhaka to act as mentors and counselors for 351 female students. These female students got life-skills training consisting of specific topics i.e. communications, leadership, and critical thinking. The female students were further organized into peerlearning groups via peer led clubs to enable transfer of skills to other students of the institution. Thus, the students became self-reliant and equipped with a transferable set of skills that helped to develop the students’ confidence and ultimately their resilience to violent extremist ideology. During the project, there were two roundtable discussions where the findings and the module developed were shared with Bangladesh Madrasah Education Board and Bangladesh Teachers Training Institute. Some journalists were also present but no press release happened due to the restrictive nature of the project.

Art and poetry workshop (2019):

On the occasion of International Women’s Day 2019, experts from the field of arts and poetry conducted workshops in Gawsia Islamia Madrasah with around 40 female students. Dibarah Mahboob facilitated the art workshop while Nandita Adhikary facilitated the poetry workshop. They were asked about their hopes and dreams and the girls produced some outstanding poems and artworks.

Lessons Learned:

English Language Development Activities received the most support from madrasahs as the students from this background are commonly known for lagging behind in English communication skills. This component served as the gateway for building relationships with the partner institutes. Besides this, activities themed on personal and menstrual hygiene were especially appealing to madrasah authorities as they coincided with Islamic values focusing on cleanliness. Workshops on soft-skills, critical thinking, climate change and first-aid were all novel and much needed initiatives in madrasahs. The Art and poetry workshops introduced the girls to media of creative expression that are inclusive of their religious sentiments, whereas the technological education and training challenged them to be innovative in a completely unfamiliar field. The madrasah teachers themselves were pleasantly surprised at what the girls achieved through both of these programmes. Among the partner institutes, some Madrasahs were considerably more cooperative in accommodating the activities of Leaping Boundaries than others. Most allowed Leaping Boundaries to intervene strictly after regular class hours, whereas others, mainly Madinatul Ulum Women’s Kamil Madrasah, would allow volunteers from Leaping Boundaries to conduct classes and workshops within class hours. The students took to the volunteers and trainers quite quickly as they were considerably more approachable than traditional madrasah teachers. While volunteers and trainers received orientation about modest dressing etiquette before conducting any activity within madrasah premises, their authorities expressed a need for more comprehensive orientations, preferably from a madrasah representative. There were several issues with absenteeism in trainings of trainers and volunteer retention in earlier years, but these were mitigated after providing makeup trainings and monetary compensation. Delays in project implementation occurred as all projects were carefully vetted by madrasah board members prior to execution, to ensure none went against Islamic values. Activities also had to be coordinated keeping schedules of volunteers (all full-time university students), Ramadan, Eid vacations and exams of madrasah students in consideration. Despite the numerous obstacles, all the students, parents and madrasah teachers wanted more of such programmes to be carried out regularly.

Further recommendations from Stakeholders:

“Islam is not just about memorising Surahs or performing Namaz, it is a comprehensive guideline for leading a proper lifestyle. There is immense scope for promoting Islamic teachings on cleanliness and personal hygiene through awareness campaigns. It is true that the curriculum mandated to us by the Bangladesh Madrasah Education Board is not sufficiently equipped to address social issues such as extremism. In order to truly expand the scope of work done in madrasas regarding such issues, the civil society and the Bangladesh Madrasah Education Board need to work together. Combined and consolidated effort is required by both sectors to build up skills of Madrasah students, male and female, to remove doubt and prejudice about their capabilities.” - Abu Noman Md. Mahbubur Rahman, Vice Principal, Gausia Islamia Fazil Madrasah

Impact Story:
“Prior to this, I could not even draw a car on a computer, let alone make one that could also move.” - Tabassum Akhter Keya, aged 16

We never had outsiders take our classes before, so I was very curious going into our first English lesson with one of the trainers from Leaping Boundaries. She was very patient and explained the lessons really well. With each passing lesson, I could gradually grasp everything that was being taught. I had the most fun learning how to program games. Prior to this, I had no idea how to draw a car on a computer, let alone make one that could also move. I don’t think I have ever felt as afraid as I did when we were told that we would have to present the games in front of an audience. It was my first time getting on a stage. I was awfully nervous and kept forgetting the lines of code. But everything worked out fine in the end. I wish we could continue practising programming because I have forgotten everything I learned. I might not be great at certain things, like painting or poetry, as many of my friends turned out to be to my surprise, I am confident about my skills with administering first aid. Before our workshop with Leaping Boundaries, we believed a little dab of toothpaste could heal everything, from cuts to burns. For some reason we were sure it could help with clotting and curing things faster. But now we know better and bring out our First Aid kits when someone is hurt. I hated missing out on a trip to Rangamati, as my madrasah didn’t permit me to go. The same thing happened after I joined the BYLC BBLT-J programme. I, along with two other girls, was forced to quit after two weeks because the classes there would stretch till the evening and we wouldn’t have been able to return to the madrasah in time for the Maghrib prayers. Even though I had my mother’s full support, I had to turn down those opportunities. Some of the fondest memories I have are with my friends during those two weeks- us travelling to BYLC together and sitting in the corner of a class where we couldn’t understand a word of English that some kids were conversing in, but feeling enthralled nonetheless.”
