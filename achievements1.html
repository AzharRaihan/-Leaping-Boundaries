Empowerment through Visibility and Representation

Shorno Kishoree (2015) :

Shorno Kishoree, the largest adolescent health network of Bangladesh, is Channel I's unique social change maker programme for promoting improved health, well being and development amongst adolescents. Students from Madinatul Ulum Women's Kamil Madrasah were represented on Shorno Kishoree to promote Adolescent Girls’ Health for Safe Motherhood. Samiya Afrin, then a student of Class 7, was selected as the Shorno Kishoree of Dhaka District.

Slant Art Exhibition (2016):

This exhibition was themed around International Women’s Day and its main focus was to shift perspectives of and about madrasahs. The exhibition was organized by Leaping Boundaries and co-hosted by Leela: where minds come to play. A total of 16 creative drawings and paintings which portrayed the student’s self-perception were showcased at the exhibition. Prior to this, a half-day art workshop was facilitated by artist Liza Hasan of Liza’s Brushes, where 57 girls from Madinatul Ulum Women’s Kamil Madrasah were asked to illustrate how they view themselves as women.

Global Dignity Day (2015-2019):

Global Dignity Day was celebrated with the support of Friendship Bangladesh where every year, 6-7 girls from affiliated madrasahs got the opportunity to share their Dignity Stories on stage in front of local and international audiences. This day celebrates what it means to be a citizen of this world with rights and respect. It defines dignity as every individual’s right to think and fend for themselves; it allows people to be independent and different but also equally respected and appreciated for the differences.

BYLC’s BBLT-J (2015-2017):

Every year, 2-3 students from Madinatul Ulum Women’s Kamil Madrasah received a fully funded scholarship from Leaping Boundaries to take part in Bangladesh Youth Leadership Center’s Building Bridges through Leadership Training Junior programme. The girls received training on improving speaking skills and were equipped with out-of-the-box strategies for coming up with useful solutions to everyday problems.

Poetry Workshop on International Women’s Day (2017):

On the occasion of international women's day, Leaping Boundaries arranged a poetry workshop at Madinatul Ulum Women's Kamil Madrasah. 60 girls participated in a workshop led by Nandita Adhikary, where they expressed their inner thoughts and dialogues through spontaneous poetry.

Toppled: A unique tech exhibition (2017):

After a rigorous 6-month training, 30 girls were selected to demonstrate games that they had programmed using their knowledge of coding at the EMK Center for public viewing. The games themselves were extraordinarily creative and addressed five different social issues.

  • For Autism, the students made an interactive game that would help autistic children discern different colours on their own.
  • For Road Safety, the students created a game that showed the red and green traffic lights and the function of these lights. They designed a computer model of a car and programmed it to stop at a red light.
  • For Women’s Empowerment, the students made a short game of a flying superwoman to represent women empowerment.
  • For Epilepsy, the students made a game where a figure having an epileptic attack would have to be saved before they fall and hurt themselves
  • For Sports and physical disability, the girls made a game where a man on a wheelchair tried to hit a cricket ball with a bat.

#freeiam Campaign (2017):

Leaping Boundaries and Breakthrough Media launched a social media campaign on Facebook with the hashtag #freeiam and introduced three madrasah girls to female role models based on what they want to be when they grow up. The objective was to break stereotypical notions around female madrasah students, help them reconcile multiple dimensions of their identities, and introduce them to the possibilities of achieving their latent dreams. The role models included women in unconventional professions i.e. a scientist, a singer and a doctor. The girls spoke to their role models and exchanged letters with them. These letters were then read out aloud. The entire process was captured on camera

Women in Leadership Festival (2018):

Leaping Boundaries set up a stall to exhibit and sell works of art and poetry made by the madrasah girls, and their achievements were featured in the magazine launched by WIL Fest.

Panel Discussion on “Innovation in the Madrasah sector during the last 5 years” (2018):

Leaping Boundaries curated a panel discussion in collaboration with the EMK Centre. The panel consisted of representatives from Madrasah Education Board, Bangladesh Madrasah Teachers Training Institute and top administrative members of institutions affiliated with Leaping Boundaries. This was an open forum where the challenges and opportunities in the development of the Madrasah sector in Bangladesh were discussed in great detail.

Dhaka Lit Fest (2018):

Sajeda Akhter, a student of Madinatul Ulum Women's Kamil madrasah, participated as a panelist in the Joy of Reading session at Dhaka Lit Fest curated by Teach For Bangladesh. The panel consisted of students from different backgrounds where they shared their love for reading books.

Girls for Peace (2018):

In collaboration with Supporting People and Rebuilding Communities (SPaRC), a 3-day residential training in Rangamati was arranged for 5 girls from madrasahs and 5 girls from the hill tracts, where they learnt and shared their cultural differences.

Mogoj Dholai 2.0 and 3.0 (2019-2020):

This competition was organised by Project Pothchola to inspire children to develop a reading habit which will lead them to become a better generation of thinkers and intellectuals. Students from Madinatul Ulum Women’s Kamil Madrasa took part in this book reading competition, and received accolades in several categories.

#YouthforSDG6 (2019):

Three students from Madinatul Ulum Women's Kamil Madrasah attended #YouthforSDG6. Youth for SDG 6 is a platform to connect youth to water, sanitation and hygiene (WASH) initiatives launched by WaterAid Bangladesh. The platform aims to provide youth with the right skills and opportunities to unleash their full potential towards supporting Bangladesh's journey towards attaining SDG 6

HerStory Reading Fellowship (2019):

Safa Khanum and Susmita Ghosh, trainers selected from the 8th batch of Leaping Boundaries trainers, were recruited for the "HerStory Reading Fellowship" by the HerStory Foundation for conducting Read Aloud Sessions in two madrasahs and discussing stories on the subject of activism, motivation, gender equality, future, career planning and self-respect. The first Read Aloud session took place at Al Amin Islamia Alim Madrasah. The fellows read out the stories of Khona, Mabia Akhtar and Preetilata Waddedar to a group of Grade 6 students followed by a discussion on leadership, courage and determination.

WOW FESTIVAL- Bites, Speed-mentoring and Marketplace (2019):

Leaping Boundaries was one of the curators of Women of the World Festival Dhaka 2019, organized by British Council. Three segments were curated - Bites, Speed-mentoring and Marketplace. The first product line of items, consisting of bookmarks, bags and scarves, inspired from the stories of the madrasah girls was launched at this event. To promote women empowerment and raise awareness, Leaping Boundaries coordinated with 18 Bites speakers, 12 speed mentors and over 50 stall owners for the marketplace.

Lessons Learned:

The Madrasahs acknowledge the lack of confidence in most students and their inability to compete with students from other educational backgrounds. Hence participation in platforms such as Global Dignity Day celebration, BYLC BBLT-, Dhaka Lit Fest, #Youth4SDG6 was encouraged by the Madrasah authorities, as long as Leaping Boundaries also arranged transportation to and from the venues. Access to leadership programmes and bootcamps tends to be limited for madrasah students due to affordability. The Slant Art exhibition was lauded by prominent personalities, directly showcasing the never-before-seen creative capabilities of female madrasah students. Toppled- a unique tech exhibition gained immense media attention and highlighted the enthusiasm of female madrasah students for learning and implementing programming skills in innovative ways. The #freeiam campaign helped the girls to identify with inspirational women excelling in fields of science and culture, and acknowledge their latent ambitions. The Girls for Peace programme was the first of its kind in Bangladesh, allowing some of the girls to travel outside of their homes and Madrasahs in Dhaka for the first time in their lives and engage in a unique cultural exchange with another marginalised community. Overall, madrasah authorities and parents witnessed a visible improvement in confidence amongst the girls.

The restrictions to the participation in such programmes arose when parents and authorities were unsure if the girls would have to give up certain aspects of their religious practices or compromise their safety in order to fit the bill. For instance, many parents were reluctant to let their daughters take part in Shorno Kishoree as the programme would be broadcasted on television and, according to some, the producers preferred representing students without face coverings (Niqab). The Panel Discussion on “Innovation in the Madrasah sector during the last 5 years” shined light on some evident contradictions between the notion of female empowerment put forth by feminism and that put forth by madrasahs in Bangladesh, and continued exploring ways of reconciling these differences with the help of government and non-governmental bodies.

pDespite logistical and scheduling issues, these activities successfully introduced the students to opportunities and people that they would otherwise not have known because their families and madrasahs lack the network, incentives and financial means required to ensure their participation in such extracurricular programmes. While the girls were always overwhelmingly enthusiastic for these programmes, their parents were unwilling to allow their daughters to participate in these platforms because they couldn’t comprehend the benefits of extracurricular activities. Both the parents and madrasah authorities also needed to be motivated for supporting such activities, whether through sharing of progress reports and press hits in Bangla (for ease of understanding) featuring Leaping Boundaries, or having the girls regularly exhibit their creative works, learnings and achievements.

Further Recommendations from Stakeholders:

“As several students in madrasahs are from impoverished backgrounds, there could have been provisions for food and rewards for incentivising them to take part in activities. Besides being a madrasah, Madinatul Ulum is primarily an orphanage. The children here will perhaps never get the chance to interact with influential people or visit extravagant buildings and locations. Even a one day event with 4-10 kids where they could experience anything they want, from meeting important personalities, to shopping or visiting amusement parks etc, could have created a ripple effect in expanding their aspirations.” - Masuda Taiyeba, teacher and chief resource person at Madinatul Ulum Women’s Kamil Madrasah

Impact Story:
“I had no idea kids my age were so active in extracurriculars.”-Samiya Afrin, aged 15

“I couldn’t have been friends with students from other educational backgrounds if Leaping Boundaries didn’t provide me with a fully funded scholarship to Bangladesh Youth Leadership Center’s BBLT-J programme, as my family would not have been able to afford it. I had no idea kids my age were so active in extracurriculars. We bonded over our shared concerns about global warming, an issue I learned about more deeply through a workshop organised by Leaping Boundaries. Besides writing and painting, I also love volunteering. Till now I’ve volunteered with Volunteer for Bangladesh, BYLC, Bangladesh Youth Volunteer and Shorno Kishoree. Starting with raising awareness on menstrual hygiene in my madrasah in 2016 to debunking myths surrounding autism within my community and, most recently, promoting proper use of masks and sanitisers amongst pedestrians along a busy road in Agargaon, I’ve thoroughly enjoyed advocating for causes I care about. If I am able to help at least one out of every ten people I speak to, it is worth my efforts. I now want to work against gender based violence in Bangladesh; not just physical violence, but also mental torture, as it can oftentimes be more debilitating for victims. For instance, the unwarranted comments men make on women’s social media posts and their effects on women’s mental health. I don’t see women in other countries being harassed online to such an extent as in Bangladesh. This year, I have been appointed as the Bangladesh Ambassador to the European Youth Symposium. Shagufe apu has been encouraging me to try for the United World Colleges, and honestly, I don’t feel even the slightest bit nervous when I think about applying abroad. My journey with Leaping Boundaries has opened my eyes to innumerous opportunities for me and my family. Previously I thought I could be happy with being a primary school teacher, but now I dream of teaching as a Professor at the University of Dhaka.”
