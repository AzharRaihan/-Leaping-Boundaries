Empowerment through Psychosocial Resilience:

Psycho-social Counselling Support (2014-2019)

Stress management, anger management, and adolescent related mental health issues were handled through psychosocial support. Symptoms of victims of abuse were identified and referred to specialists as necessary. To begin with, the students were counselled in groups of four. As special cases were identified, one on one counselling were provided on a need basis. The Trainers were trained in providing psychosocial support by an expert in Educational Counselling Psychology.

World Mental Health Day (2015)

Volunteers from the Psychosocial Support component talked to the girls at Madinatul Ulum about Emotional Hygiene and how it is important to take care of the mind as well as the body

World Mental Health Day (2017)

Volunteers from the Psychosocial Counselling Component raised awareness on depression, stress management and mental hygiene through workshops in madrasahs with both students and teachers. They engaged the students in role-play exercises for them to grasp the concept of depression and exchanged stories afterwards. The partner madrasahs were grossly understaffed; their teachers poorly were compensated and possessed a high level of occupational stress.

Workshops on Taming Inner Shadows (2018)

Leaping Boundaries ran workshops in five madrasahs across Dhaka city. 16-19 year old girls were taught about taming their inner shadows by defining boundaries, gender, power and body language, handling stress, depression and anger, managing multiple dimensions of identities and leading peacefully. The girls then painted their shadows, their hopes, their conceptualization of gender, religion, and identity on paper.

World Mental Health Month (2019):

To mark World Mental Health Month, Leaping Boundaries, in collaboration with Mongoldip Foundation, conducted a series of awareness sessions in different institutions of Dhaka. The first session was conducted in Bongobondhu Sheikh Mujib Academy by one of the Leaping Boundaries Changemakers, Risalat Binte Awlad. The second session was conducted by another Changemaker, Faheema Khanam in Moghbazar Girls High School. Niger Sultana supported the programme as an expert consultant.

Responding to Gender Based Violence during COVID 19: Supporting Female Madrasah Scholars in Bangladesh (2020):

As part of this project, 70 girls were provided with para-counseling sessions over telephone every week for three months to help them unload stress and learn better coping skills. They were also provided with psychoeducation on adolescence, domestic violence and related helplines. The parents/guardians also received information sessions focusing on conflict and anger management and how to foster better family bonding. Additional activities included distribution of pamphlets on domestic violence and child abuse as awareness raising material to the families of the project beneficiaries. The total number of beneficiaries reached during this project was 324, including the household members of the 70 madrasah girls. A series of posts on similar pamphlet contents were also published on the Leaping Boundaries social media pages. These had a total reach of 216,400.

Impact Story:
"Even now when I recall such incidents, I take the time to pause and breathe deeply. This calms me down everytime I feel sad or angry.”- Afroza Akhter, 17, Madinatul Ulum Women's Kamil Madrasah

I would get really excited for the visits from the trainers. They were like our friends. We were never as open with our usual teachers at the Madrasah. I had no prior experience with psychosocial counseling before meeting one of the Psychosocial support volunteers when I was in Grade 7. At first we all shared our personal thoughts anonymously and then when I spoke to her directly, it was as if she understood my deepest thoughts, without ever having met me before. I wasn't aware of it back then but now I understand that I had some deep seated anger issues. We did meditation and breathing exercises together. I still practice these, actively reminding myself to pause and breathe whenever I come across triggers and regrets. I become very angry every time someone catcalls me in the street. Once as I was talking to a friend beside an alley, a couple of boys who had been ogling at us started singing loudly. One of them said to the other, “Maybe we should stop singing, they are clearly not interested.” What the boy told in response still brings my blood to boil- “Or maybe they ARE interested and that’s why they aren’t telling us to stop.” I remember going up to them and shouting my head off, only then they backed off. My mother is terribly afraid of my temper, worried that it could land me in trouble some day. Even now when I recall such incidents, I take the time to pause and breathe deeply. This calms me down everytime I feel sad or angry. My parents are very conservative, but they have both been quietly supportive of my education and hobbies. However, because I couldn’t resume education during the pandemic, my father had made the decision to marry me off and started exploring suitors. I became very upset and didn’t know what to do. I told my friends about this and together we hatched an elaborate plan to thwart any suitor from pursuing talks of marriage with my parents. And let’s just say that it worked. Afterwards, not without considerable deliberation with my father, I was able to persuade him to continue to support my education.
